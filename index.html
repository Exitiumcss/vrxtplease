    The lens: you’re not “giving admin,” you’re buying capacity

    Better permissions for the right person reduce your load: fewer tickets stuck on you, faster cleanups, calmer voice chats, events that actually ship.

    When ASAP asks for more access, the useful question isn’t “Do they deserve power?” — it’s “What recurring work will leave my plate if they can do it safely?” Moderation queues, channel hygiene, onboarding flows, bot hygiene, and incident triage are all skills. If ASAP already demonstrates judgment in public threads, that’s capacity you can unlock.

    The best owners don’t hoard every toggle — they distribute least privilege with clear boundaries so one person isn’t the bottleneck at 2 a.m.

    Elevating ASAP doesn’t have to mean the Administrator checkbox. Often the better move is a custom role: stronger than “trusted member,” weaker than “god mode,” with a name that explains itself in the member list (e.g. Senior Mod · Events).

    If you’ve already seen them de-escalate drama, document decisions, and ping staff privately instead of farming chaos in general chat — that’s the behavioral signal that more permission is an investment, not a gamble.

    Note for ASAP: if you’re the one sharing this page, stay quiet while they read. Let the structure do the talking.

    Administrator vs “better perms” (scoped)

    You can give ASAP more meaningful authority without handing them the one toggle that bypasses most safeguards.

    Blanket Administrator

    • Huge blast radius if account is lost or rushed
    • Harder to explain to members (“why do they have god?”)
    • Often more than the work actually requires

    Scoped elevation

    • Matches real tasks: mod, events, bots, category builds
    • Readable role name + pinned staff rationale
    • Easier to expand later than to walk back a disaster

    PermissionOwner lens

    If you’re leaning “yes” — stack safeguards first

    1. Role position: keep new power below your top staff role so hierarchy can’t be self-edited upward by mistake.
    2. Name the role like a job: “ASAP · Senior Mod” reads clearer than “Admin 2.”
    3. Pinned staff policy: one message: what they can do solo vs what needs a second pair of eyes.
    4. Backup mod: ensure someone else is awake in overlapping windows — nobody should be lone-wolf with ban perms during drama spikes.
    1. Trial window: “30 days at Tier B, review on [date]” — reduces pressure to decide forever today.
    2. Office hours: heavier tools only when senior staff is around — optional but sane for newer servers.
    3. Retro: quick staff thread after first week: what broke, what didn’t, what to add/remove.
